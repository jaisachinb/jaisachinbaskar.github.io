Product Thinking

Where Discovery Breaks for AI Products
Traditional product discovery assumes users have intent. What happens when the product acts before they do?

Most PM discovery frameworks — Jobs-to-be-Done, user story mapping, usability testing — were designed for reactive interfaces. Agentic AI products infer goals, execute autonomously, and produce outputs the user never explicitly requested. That breaks the standard discovery loop. PMs building in this space need to redefine what "user need" means, how to measure success, and how to design for trust and control simultaneously. I'm actively working through what a new discovery playbook looks like for AI-native products.

SaaS → AaaS: The Outcome Shift
When software stops being a tool and starts being an agent, who is actually doing the work?

SaaS charged you for access. AI as a Service charges for outcomes. That sounds like progress until you ask: what happens when the outcome is wrong? How do you price something you can't fully predict? And how do product teams research user needs when users themselves don't fully understand what the AI did? These aren't theoretical — they're the next five years of product design.

Accountability Without Humans
If an AI makes a decision that harms someone, and no human approved it — who is responsible?

My work at the University of Sydney on AI adoption and platform governance sits directly in this question. Accountability is only meaningful when it can be assigned, enforced, and felt. As AI systems absorb more decision-making — in hiring, lending, healthcare, content moderation — the gap between "the algorithm decided" and "someone is responsible" becomes a product problem, a legal problem, and a trust problem simultaneously. I think about how product teams design for this accountability gap before regulators force them to.

Discovery for AI-Native Products
Usability testing assumes a user doing something. What do you test when the AI is doing it for them?

Classic discovery methods — think-alouds, task completion, interviews — were built for interfaces that respond to user input. Agentic AI systems don't wait for input. They infer intent, act autonomously, and produce outputs users didn't explicitly request. Product teams building AI-native products need entirely new frameworks for evaluating trust, comprehension, and perceived control. This is the product methodology gap I find most compelling right now — and the one most teams are quietly ignoring.

AI Is Reshaping Every Vertical — Product Is at the Centre
Product × Health AI

AI is detecting haemorrhages, flagging tumours, and triaging ER patients — I've published in this space directly with my work on automated intracranial haemorrhage detection. But the harder product question isn't whether the AI is accurate. It's: does the clinician trust it enough to act on it? Does the interface surface uncertainty in a way the user can interpret? When AI assists a diagnosis, the product design determines whether that assistance helps or harms.

Product × Future of Work

The real question for work-tool PMs isn't whether AI replaces jobs — it's whether the products we build for AI-assisted work keep humans in meaningful control. From my work at the University of Sydney, I saw how workers adapt their behaviour around AI tools in ways product teams never anticipated: over-trust, under-trust, workarounds. Getting that collaboration design right is the central product challenge of the next decade.

Product × Trust & Safety

Safety in AI products isn't just about preventing harm — it's about whether users can recognise when something has gone wrong. My metaverse platform work exposed how AI-driven environments create new product risks: misinformation that feels authoritative, decisions made without user awareness, manufactured trust. Building for safety in AI-native products means designing for legibility first — that's a product problem before it's a policy one.
