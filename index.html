Currently Exploring

AI & The Future of Consulting
If AI can analyse faster, synthesise better, and never sleeps — what exactly is a consultant selling?

The consulting industry is built on expertise, judgment, and trust. AI threatens the first, augments the second, and makes the third more complicated. I'm exploring where human consultants retain irreplaceable value — and where they're already being replaced without anyone admitting it. The answer probably lives in the space between pattern recognition and genuine judgment under ambiguity.

SaaS → AaaS: The Outcome Shift
When software stops being a tool and starts being an agent, who is actually doing the work?

SaaS charged you for access. AI as a Service charges for outcomes. That sounds like progress until you ask: what happens when the outcome is wrong? How do you price something you can't fully predict? And how do product teams research user needs when users themselves don't fully understand what the AI did? These aren't theoretical — they're the next five years of product design.

Accountability Without Humans
If an AI makes a decision that harms someone, and no human approved it — who is responsible?

My research at the University of Sydney on AI adoption and platform governance sits directly in this question. Accountability is only meaningful when it can be assigned, enforced, and felt. As AI systems absorb more decision-making — in hiring, lending, healthcare, content moderation — the gap between "the algorithm decided" and "someone is responsible" becomes a product problem, a legal problem, and a trust problem simultaneously. I think about how platforms design for this, and how researchers should be measuring it.

Research Methods for AI-Native Products
Usability testing assumes a user doing something. What do you test when the AI is doing it for them?

Classic research methods — think-alouds, task completion, interviews — were built for interfaces that respond to input. Agentic AI systems don't wait for input. They infer intent, act autonomously, and produce outputs users didn't explicitly request. Evaluating trust, comprehension, and control in these systems requires entirely new frameworks. This is the methodological gap I find most interesting right now.

AI Is Reshaping Every Field — And HCI Is at the Centre
Human – AI × Health

AI is detecting haemorrhages, flagging tumours, and triaging ER patients — I've published in this space directly with my work on automated intracranial haemorrhage detection. But the harder question isn't whether the AI is accurate. It's: does the clinician trust it enough to act on it? Does the patient understand what made the decision? When AI assists a diagnosis, human accountability doesn't disappear — it becomes harder to locate. HCI research has to answer where the human stays in the loop, and what that loop even looks like.

Human – AI × Future of Work

The question isn't whether AI replaces jobs — it's whether the interfaces we build for AI-assisted work actually keep humans in meaningful control. From my research on AI adoption at the University of Sydney, I saw how workers adapt their behaviour around AI tools in ways designers never anticipated. People over-trust, under-trust, or work around the system entirely. The future of work isn't just about automation — it's about designing collaboration between humans and AI systems that doesn't quietly erode human skill, judgment, or agency.

Human – AI × User Safety

Safety in AI systems isn't just about preventing harm — it's about whether users can even recognise when something has gone wrong. My metaverse platform research exposed how AI-driven environments create new categories of risk: misinformation that feels authoritative, decisions made on your behalf without your awareness, and trust that's manufactured rather than earned. User safety in AI-native products requires designing for legibility — systems where users can understand, question, and override what the AI is doing. That's an HCI problem before it's anything else.
